Attribution

The shoulders we stand on.

Yander is built on the work of many open communities. This page lists every external data source, the licence under which we use it, and the credits we display.

Last updated 15 June 2026 Open data & open source

We aim to go beyond the strict legal minimum where the spirit of a licence asks us to. For a quick reference, the short attribution strings required on the map and on relevant UI surfaces are reproduced verbatim in Section 6. Where a project requests a specific wording, we use it exactly.

If you spot an attribution we are missing or have got wrong, please email legal@yander.app — we will fix it as quickly as we can.

1. OpenStreetMap

What we use it for

OpenStreetMap is the primary source of the road, path, water and place data behind every Yander route. We use OpenStreetMap data for:

  • the underlying road and footpath graph that our routing engines (OSRM and Valhalla) operate on;
  • searching, displaying and describing places (points of interest, parks, rivers, lakes, towns);
  • map tiles outside of Great Britain, served by community and commercial OSM tile providers;
  • enriching saved routes with names, surface, tracktype and other tags.

Licence

  • Data: Open Database Licence 1.0 (ODbL). Full text: opendatacommons.org/licenses/odbl/1-0/
  • Cartography: generally Creative Commons BY-SA 2.0, per the OpenStreetMap Foundation's cartography licence note.

Required attribution

© OpenStreetMap contributors

We show this credit on every map surface where OSM data is visible (bottom-right of the map), in this Attribution page, and in our Terms of Service. The phrase links to openstreetmap.org/copyright in the live app, as recommended by the OSM Foundation.

Compliance commitments

  • We do not redistribute raw OSM data. Yander queries the road graph at runtime; users cannot bulk-export OSM tiles or geometries from the app.
  • Any derivative database we publish in future (for example, a curated public dataset of scenic routes) will itself be published under ODbL, in line with the share-alike requirement.
  • Where we display OSM data alongside non-OSM data (for example, OS feature density), we make clear which source each element comes from.

2. OSRM (Open Source Routing Machine)

What we use it for

OSRM is the primary routing engine for driving, walking and cycling routes inside Yander. We run the public OSRM demo endpoints and self-hosted OSRM instances against the OpenStreetMap graph.

Licence

OSRM: BSD 2-Clause Licence. Full text: github.com/Project-OSRM/osrm-backend.

Attribution

The BSD 2-Clause Licence requires us to retain the OSRM copyright notice in any redistribution of the OSRM source code, but does not require user-facing attribution. We nevertheless credit OSRM here and in our Terms of Service because we believe attribution is good practice when a project does this much heavy lifting for us.

Routing by OSRM (project-osrm.org)

3. Valhalla

What we use it for

Valhalla is our secondary routing engine. We use Valhalla as a fallback when OSRM is unavailable or times out, and for multi-stop routing, isochrones and certain scenic-cost optimisations that we have authored on top of Valhalla's customisable costing.

Licence

Valhalla: MIT Licence. Full text: github.com/valhalla/valhalla.

Attribution

The MIT Licence requires us to retain the copyright notice in any redistribution of the source code, but does not require user-facing attribution. We nevertheless credit Valhalla here and in our Terms of Service.

Routing by Valhalla (valhalla.github.io)

4. Ordnance Survey (OS Data Hub)

What we use it for

Within Great Britain, we use the OS Data Hub for two purposes:

  • OS Maps API — raster map tiles in OS Outdoor, Light and Road styles, served via our server-side proxy so that the OS Data Hub key is never exposed to the browser;
  • OS Features API and OS NGD API – Features — feature data (greenspace, national parks, paths and roads) used to score the walk-friendliness of a route ("OS-informed" suitability labels such as "Path-friendly" or "Mostly streets").

We do not use OS data outside Great Britain.

Licence

Use is governed by our OS Data Hub subscription and the OS Open Data Licence / OS Data Hub Terms and Conditions. See osdatahub.os.uk/legal.

Required attribution

Contains OS data © Crown copyright and database right 2026

We show this credit on every map view where OS tiles are visible, alongside any OS-derived UI label, in this Attribution page, and in our Terms of Service. The string is generated server-side by /api/os/config so it stays in lockstep with the year and any future wording changes required by OS.

Compliance commitments

  • We do not redistribute raw OS data or OS tiles. Tiles are served on-the-fly through our proxy for the duration of a user session only, and are not bulk-cached for offline reuse.
  • OS-derived feature counts are summarised into honest scores; we never present OS data as if it were the user's own.
  • We respect any rate limits and entitlement boundaries set by OS Data Hub.

5. Wikipedia and Wikimedia Commons

What we use it for

Wikipedia and Wikimedia Commons help us tell the story of the places along a Yander route:

  • short "extract" summaries shown when you tap a point of interest or epic-drive stop, fetched live from the Wikipedia REST API;
  • thumbnail and full-size images for places and landmarks, fetched live from Wikipedia article images, Wikidata's P18 claim, and the Wikimedia Commons search and image-info APIs.

Licence

  • Wikipedia article text (including the "extract" summaries we use) is licensed under Creative Commons Attribution–ShareAlike 4.0 International (CC BY-SA 4.0). Full text: creativecommons.org/licenses/by-sa/4.0.
  • Wikimedia Commons media is licensed on a per-file basis. The most common licences are CC BY-SA (various versions), CC BY (various versions), CC0 / public domain, and the GNU Free Documentation Licence. Each file's licence and credit information is available from its file description page on commons.wikimedia.org.

Required attribution

For Wikipedia text excerpts we display:

From the Wikipedia article "<article title>" by Wikipedia contributors, licensed under CC BY-SA 4.0.

The article title links to the canonical Wikipedia page, satisfying CC BY-SA 4.0's requirements to attribute the work to its author, link to the work, name the licence and link to its text, and indicate that the material has been modified if we have edited it (we sometimes truncate to a single sentence — we describe this on the page).

For Commons images we display, alongside the image:

Image: <file title>, by <author or "Wikimedia Commons contributors">, licensed under <licence>.

The file title links to the canonical Commons file page so anyone can audit the original licence terms and full attribution. If a particular file requires GFDL or a different specific phrasing, we follow the wording the uploader requests.

ShareAlike implications

CC BY-SA 4.0's share-alike clause means that if we adapt a Wikipedia article extract, that adapted text must also be made available under CC BY-SA 4.0 or a compatible licence. Where Yander shows AI-generated descriptions that may themselves have been informed by Wikipedia content, we treat the AI output as inspired by, but distinct from, Wikipedia text — and we never present AI-generated paragraphs as if they were the Wikipedia article. We label every direct Wikipedia extract clearly as such, with the attribution above.

7. Other dependencies

Yander stands on the shoulders of many open-source projects beyond those whose licences require attribution. We are grateful for, among others:

  • MapLibre GL (rendering)
  • Leaflet (legacy rendering, being retired)
  • React, Express, Drizzle ORM, Passport.js (application stack)
  • Overpass API (OSM querying)
  • Nominatim (geocoding)
  • OpenFreeMap (non-GB vector tiles)

Their respective licences and copyright notices are preserved in our source code distribution.

8. Changes to this page

We may update this page when we add or remove a data source, when a licence changes, or when a project asks for different wording. The "Last updated" date at the top reflects the most recent revision. We will not remove an attribution as long as the underlying data is still being used.
